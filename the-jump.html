Phase 1 — The Approach

THE IN-RUN

Deep aerodynamic tuck at 90 km/h. Chin nearly touching the knees, hands streamlined ahead.

Phase 2 — Liftoff

THE TAKEOFF

Explosive extension from the lip in just 0.3 seconds — timing is everything.

Phase 3 — Going Airborne

THE TRANSITION

Body pitches forward into flight position. Skis begin to open into the V-formation.

Phase 4 — Peak Flight

SOARING

Body flat as a wing, skis wide in V-style. Top jumpers fly over 140 meters on large hills.

Phase 5 — Touchdown

THE TELEMARK LANDING

One foot forward, knees bent, arms wide — maximum style points from the judges.

Helmet
Aerodynamic shell reduces drag up to 8%. Custom-molded to each athlete.
Jump Suit
Within 2cm of body measurements. Fabric permeability tested by FIS officials.
Skis
Up to 146% of body height. Wide, light, with upturned tips — designed for lift.
Boots & Bindings
Only the toe binds to the ski. Flexible ankle for the forward lean in flight.
Tuck Position
Minimizes frontal area. Athletes hit 90 km/h on the in-run.
V-Style Formation
28% more lift than parallel. Invented by Jan Boklöv in 1985.
Body Angle
40–45 degree forward lean turns the body into a lifting surface — like an aircraft wing.
Telemark
One ski forward, one back, deeply bent. Worth up to 20 style points.
